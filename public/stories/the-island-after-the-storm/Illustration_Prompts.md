# Illustration Prompt Guide: The Island After the Storm

## Master Style

MASTER STYLE: Vibrant, warm children's book illustration in soft digital painting style. Clean outlines with rich saturated colors. The child narrator is a Dominican girl around age 7, with warm brown skin, thick black hair in two puff buns, wearing a yellow sundress with white trim. Her Mamá is a tall Dominican woman with warm brown skin, black curly hair pulled back, wearing a floral blouse and denim shorts. Her Papá is a broad-shouldered Dominican man with deep brown skin, short black hair, wearing a white sleeveless shirt and khaki pants. Doña Carmen is an elderly Dominican woman with silver hair, warm brown wrinkled skin, and a bright blue housedress. Tío René is a stocky Dominican man with dark brown skin, a mustache, and a red baseball cap. The setting is a colorful Caribbean neighborhood in the Dominican Republic with painted concrete homes in coral, yellow, and turquoise, lush tropical plants, and a warm golden-orange sun. 16:9 landscape format.

## Page Prompts

### Page 1 — `page-01.webp`
> Last night, the hurricane roared over our island like a giant, angry drum.

A dramatic nighttime scene over a Caribbean island neighborhood, dark storm clouds swirling in violent purple and grey spirals across the sky, fierce winds bending tall palm trees sideways, rain slashing diagonally across colorful concrete homes painted coral and turquoise. Lightning flashes illuminate the chaotic sky, casting silver light on flooding streets below. The scene is viewed from slightly above, showing the full force of the hurricane roaring over the village with tremendous energy. No characters are visible — only the raw, overwhelming power of the storm over the small neighborhood. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 2 — `page-02.webp`
> This morning, I opened our door and saw our street covered in broken branches and mud.

A Dominican girl age 7 with warm brown skin and thick black hair in two puff buns, wearing a yellow sundress, stands in the open doorway of a coral-painted concrete home, her eyes wide with shock and sadness. She looks out at a street buried in snapped branches, torn leaves, and rivers of muddy brown water flowing between colorful houses. Debris is scattered everywhere — broken fences, overturned flowerpots, sheets of corrugated metal twisted across the road. Soft morning light in muted gold and grey filters through the cloudy post-storm sky. The girl's expression is a mixture of awe and quiet sorrow. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 3 — `page-03.webp`
> Our beautiful mango tree, the one Papá climbed every summer, had fallen across the yard.

A large mango tree with a thick trunk has toppled and crashed across a lush Caribbean backyard, its roots torn from the red earth, leaves and green mangoes scattered across the muddy ground. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress stands beside her Papá, a broad-shouldered man with deep brown skin in a white sleeveless shirt and khaki pants, both staring at the fallen tree with expressions of deep sadness. Papá rests one gentle hand on the trunk of the tree, as if grieving an old friend. The yard behind a coral-painted house is filled with soft morning light, the broken tree dominating the scene. The mood is mournful, quiet, and tender. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 4 — `page-04.webp`
> Across the road, Doña Carmen stood in front of her house with no roof above her head.

Across a debris-scattered Caribbean street, an elderly Dominican woman named Doña Carmen stands in front of her turquoise concrete home, her silver hair loose, wearing a bright blue housedress, staring upward with a weary expression. The entire roof of her house is missing — only exposed wooden rafters and torn edges remain, open to the pale morning sky. Shards of corrugated roofing lie scattered in the yard and road around her. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress watches from across the road, her hand over her mouth in surprise. The scene is heartbreaking but rendered in warm, soft tones to remain accessible for young readers. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 5 — `page-05.webp`
> Mamá squeezed my hand and said, "We are safe, and that is the most important thing."

Inside a bright coral-painted Caribbean home, the Dominican girl in the yellow sundress stands close to her Mamá, a tall woman with warm brown skin, black curly hair pulled back, wearing a floral blouse and denim shorts. Mamá kneels down to the girl's height and tenderly squeezes both of the girl's hands in hers, looking into her eyes with a calm, loving, reassuring expression. The girl's face shifts from worry to quiet comfort, a small hopeful smile beginning to form. Warm golden morning light streams through a window behind them, illuminating dust motes in the air. Framed family photos on the wall behind them are slightly tilted but intact. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 6 — `page-06.webp`
> All through our neighborhood, families stepped outside and looked at each other with tired, hopeful eyes.

A wide view of a colorful Caribbean neighborhood street after the hurricane, showing six or seven Dominican families emerging from their painted concrete homes in shades of coral, yellow, turquoise, and green. Adults and children of all ages stand in doorways and front yards, looking at each other across the debris-covered street with expressions that are tired but softly hopeful — eyes meeting eyes, quiet nods, hands raised in gentle waves. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress stands with her Mamá and Papá, all three looking outward toward the community. The morning light is warm amber and gold, beginning to break through the clouds. The scene conveys shared exhaustion and the fragile beginning of solidarity. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 7 — `page-07.webp`
> Papá and our neighbor Tío René picked up their machetes and began to clear the fallen tree together.

In a sunlit Caribbean backyard beside a fallen mango tree, Papá — a broad-shouldered Dominican man with deep brown skin in a white sleeveless shirt — swings a machete powerfully into a thick branch, his face set with determined concentration. Beside him, Tío René, a stocky Dominican man with dark brown skin, a mustache, and a red baseball cap, hacks at another branch with his own machete, grinning with effort. Wood chips fly, and a growing pile of cleared branches sits to the side. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress watches from nearby, her expression shifting from sadness to admiration. The scene is full of energy, motion, and the warm colors of a bright post-storm morning. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 8 — `page-08.webp`
> Mamá filled a big pot with rice and beans and carried it to the street for everyone to share.

On a sunny Caribbean street lined with colorful houses, Mamá — a tall Dominican woman with warm brown skin in a floral blouse and denim shorts — stands beside a steaming large blue enamel pot set on an outdoor fire, stirring rice and beans with a long wooden spoon. She smiles warmly as she ladles generous portions into bowls and plates handed to her by a line of grateful neighbors of all ages — elderly, young, children — who wait with tired but grateful faces. The aroma of food seems to fill the air, shown through soft illustrated steam curling upward. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress stands beside her Mamá helping to pass plates. The scene radiates warmth, generosity, and community. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 9 — `page-09.webp`
> The children on our block passed cups of cold water to the grown-ups working hard in the hot sun.

On a bright sun-drenched Caribbean street, a group of children ages 5 to 10 with warm brown skin in colorful clothing carry plastic cups and a large pitcher of water, moving between sweaty adults who are clearing debris and chopping wood in the intense afternoon heat. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress leads the group, carefully passing a full cup of water up to a tall adult man, her face earnest and proud. The adults accept the water gratefully, mopping their brows and smiling down at the children. The sun is blazing white-gold overhead, and long shadows fall across the rubble-strewn street. The mood is purposeful, warm, and joyful. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 10 — `page-10.webp`
> By afternoon, a long line of neighbors climbed up onto Doña Carmen's roof with hammers and new wood.

A wide, dynamic scene of a Caribbean neighborhood street showing a long line of ten or more Dominican neighbors of varied ages climbing ladders and working together on top of Doña Carmen's turquoise house, passing planks of fresh golden-yellow wood hand to hand, hammering nails, and measuring beams under a bright blue sky with a few white clouds. Doña Carmen in her bright blue housedress watches from below with her hands clasped over her heart and tears of gratitude in her eyes. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress stands in the crowd below looking up, amazed. The scene is filled with movement, color, and collective energy, all the figures working in joyful harmony. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 11 — `page-11.webp`
> I watched strong hands lifting, building, and fixing, and I felt something warm growing inside my chest.

A close, intimate scene focused on the Dominican girl in the yellow sundress, standing still in the middle of a bustling work scene, her eyes wide and glistening with wonder as she watches the hands of many neighbors around her — dark brown hands swinging hammers, strong hands lifting beams, gentle hands passing nails, careful hands steadying ladders. The camera angle is low, from her eye level, making the working adults look tall and powerful all around her. The girl presses one hand to her chest over her heart, a radiant expression of awe and warmth spreading across her face. Golden afternoon light falls across the entire scene like something sacred. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 12 — `page-12.webp`
> Three days later, Doña Carmen had a new roof, and every street on our block was clean and clear.

A joyful wide-angle view of a colorful Dominican Caribbean neighborhood three days after the storm, streets completely clean and swept, every house intact, flowers blooming again in vivid pinks and oranges in rebuilt garden beds. Doña Carmen stands proudly in front of her turquoise house, which now has a bright new roof gleaming in the sunlight, raising her hands in celebration while neighbors cheer around her. The Dominican girl in the yellow sundress, Mamá in her floral blouse, and Papá in his white sleeveless shirt all stand together smiling broadly, arms around each other. The entire block glows in warm golden light under a perfectly clear blue sky. The mood is triumphant, joyful, and deeply satisfying. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.

### Page 13 — `page-13.webp`
> Standing under the blue sky, I knew that no storm could ever blow away the love of our community.

A beautiful final scene: the Dominican girl in the yellow sundress stands alone in the center of a wide, clean Caribbean street, head tilted back, arms slightly open at her sides, gazing up at a vast brilliant blue sky with small white clouds drifting peacefully. Behind her, the entire colorful neighborhood stretches in both directions — coral, turquoise, and yellow homes, green tropical trees, neighbors waving and chatting on porches, children playing, Mamá and Papá visible in the distance smiling at her. The girl's expression is pure, quiet joy and deep knowing — a child who has understood something important about love and strength. Warm golden sunlight pours over everything, and the scene glows with hope, beauty, and peace. Children's book illustration, 16:9 landscape.
